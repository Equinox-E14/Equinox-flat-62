The area

Greener than
you'd expect.

The Isle of Dogs is bounded by water on three sides. That geography gives it something unusual for inner London: genuine open space, a working city farm, and a river walk on the doorstep.

Island Gardens Adjacent
The same panorama of the Naval College that Canaletto painted in the 1750s — legally protected, immediately next door.
Greenwich Foot Tunnel 5 min walk
A Victorian tunnel under the Thames, open around the clock, emerging directly below the Cutty Sark.
Mudchute Park & Farm 10 min walk
32 acres of nature reserve with one of Europe's largest city farms. Canary Wharf towers over the meadow. It never gets old.
Millwall Park 8 min walk
The Island's main green space — sports pitches, a playground, cherry blossom in April, and a direct path to Mudchute.

The Thames Path National Trail runs the entire north bank of the Isle of Dogs loop — from Masthouse Terrace, past the Millwall riverfront where Brunel's SS Great Eastern was launched sideways into the river, past the Docklands Sailing Centre, and around to Canary Wharf. River light, changing skies, and the Greenwich skyline across the water. The full loop is approximately 7 km and entirely flat. It is a genuinely exceptional place to start or end a day.

Greenwich · Direct by DLR or Foot Tunnel

A World Heritage
Site as your weekend.

Most E14 listings mention Greenwich. Few make clear how close it is — or how good.

Twenty years of Friday mornings through the Foot Tunnel. It never felt like a commute. It always felt like an escape.

Greenwich Park 10 min
180 acres of Royal Park — London's oldest. Climb to the Observatory and you're standing on the prime meridian. This property sits seconds from that line in the Eastern Hemisphere.
Cutty Sark 10 min
The world's last surviving tea clipper, fully restored, dry-docked on the Greenwich riverfront. Visible from Island Gardens across the water on a clear day.
Greenwich Market 10 min
London's only market in a UNESCO World Heritage Site. 50+ street food stalls, arts and crafts, Victorian covered hall. Friday to Sunday.
Old Royal Naval College 10 min
Wren's 1712 baroque masterwork. The Painted Hall — the Sistine Chapel of the UK — took 19 years to paint. Free to walk the grounds; Painted Hall ticketed.

Inigo Jones's 1635 Palladian villa — Britain's first classical building — is free to enter, housing the National Maritime Museum's fine art collection. The NMM itself is the world's largest maritime museum. Both free.

Each summer, ocean liners dock at the Greenwich Ship Tier below the Cutty Sark. From Island Gardens — directly opposite — watching a full-scale liner moored beside a Victorian clipper and Wren's baroque college is a view that stops people mid-stride. It appears on no tourist map.

A sense of place

The Island
beneath the glass.

Canary Wharf is recent. The Isle of Dogs is ancient. Both are visible from the terrace.

This property sits in the Eastern Hemisphere. The prime meridian runs through Greenwich Observatory, directly across the water. From the front door, you are seconds from the line.

Long before Equinox, this exact plot was the terminus of the Millwall Extension Railway (1872). Passengers stepped off steam trains and walked to a river jetty, where the Greenwich Ferry carried them across to the south bank. The railway returned in 1987 as Island Gardens DLR, built on the original viaduct route. In 1999 when the DLR tunnelled beneath the Thames, the old station made way for Equinox. Living here means occupying a peaceful piece of London's transit history.

The adjacent waterfront was preserved in the 19th century by the Commissioners of the Royal Hospital, who purchased the ground specifically to protect the view across the Thames — the same view Canaletto painted in the 1750s. It is legally protected. It always will be.

c.1800s

West India Docks open — engine room of Britain's Caribbean trade. Walls still stand, now the Docklands Museum.

1860s

Brunel's SS Great Eastern launched sideways into the Thames from the Millwall riverfront, adjacent to this site.

Island Gardens opens. The Canaletto view — now the park immediately next door.

1902

Greenwich Foot Tunnel opens — still one of London's most atmospheric short journeys.

1987–99

Island Gardens DLR opens, tunnels beneath the Thames. Equinox built on the old station footprint.
